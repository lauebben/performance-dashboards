Metric Glossary
What each measurement means in practice
Jump Height (cm)
The vertical displacement of the center of mass during a countermovement jump, measured from flight time. A primary indicator of lower-body explosive power. Reductions of >10% from personal best may signal neuromuscular fatigue.
mRSI — modified Reactive Strength Index (m/s)
Jump height divided by time on the force plate (contact time). Measures how explosively an athlete generates force relative to the time taken. High mRSI = reactive, elastic power. Sensitive to fatigue: drops before jump height does.
Propulsive Mean Force (kg)
The average force applied during the propulsive (push-off) phase of the jump. Reflects overall force production capacity. Lower values indicate reduced muscular output, often linked to accumulated fatigue or poor readiness.
Propulsive Peak Force (kg)
The maximum force produced during the propulsive phase. Indicates peak neuromuscular drive. A drop in peak force with maintained mean force can signal a shift from explosive to grind-style force production — an early fatigue marker.
Time to Take Off (ms)
Total time from movement initiation to leaving the force plate. Shorter values indicate a more explosive, efficient jump strategy. Increases in this metric suggest the athlete is taking longer to generate force — a key fatigue indicator.
Time to Peak Force (ms)
Time from the start of the propulsive phase to reaching maximum force. A fast time to peak force reflects high rate of force development (RFD). When fatigued, athletes reach peak force later — reducing explosive effectiveness in sprinting, jumping and change of direction.
Propulsive Duration (ms)
The duration of the upward push-off phase. Shorter propulsive duration combined with high force = greater power output. Longer durations suggest the athlete is struggling to produce force quickly, often a sign of neuromuscular fatigue or reduced readiness.
Landing Impulse (kg·s)
The force-time integral during the landing phase. Represents total braking load absorbed by the body on landing. High values indicate hard, stiff landings with greater joint stress. Monitoring trends helps assess injury risk and neuromuscular control quality.
Fatigue Score methodology: Composite of Propulsive Duration, Time to Take Off, Time to Peak Force and mRSI — each weighted equally at 25%. Time-based metrics are inverted (lower = fresher). All values are normalised to the athlete's own personal range (0–100 scale). ● Fresh >80   ● Caution 60–80   ● Fatigued <60
